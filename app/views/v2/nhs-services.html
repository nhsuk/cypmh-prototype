If you’re struggling with how you are feeling (your mental health) and feel like you need support, you’re not alone.

The NHS provides mental health services for children and young people who need professional support to manage their mental health.

{% include "includes/urgent-care-card.html" %}

How do children and young people’s mental health services work?

Children and young people’s mental health services (CYMPHS) cover a wide range of mental health issues and offer treatment such as talking therapies, family therapy and medicines.

You may also hear these services called children and adolescent mental health services (CAMHS).

Services might be different depending on where you live. But the steps to getting help are usually the same for everyone. These are:

  1. Step 1 Getting referred

    To use NHS specialist mental health services, you will usually need a referral from a GP, teacher, social worker or youth worker. In some cases you may also be able to refer yourself (or your parent or carer can refer you).

    Find out more about how you can get a referral

  2. Step 2 Getting assessed

    After you’ve been referred, the mental health service will look at your personal information and decide what support you might need (this is called triage). They may get in touch with you, or the person who referred you, to find out more information to help them make that decision.

    You may then be offered an appointment to have an assessment with a specialist. Or you may be given information and resources so you can do things on your own to help yourself, called self-management.

    Find out more about triage and assessment

  3. Step 3 Treatment

    Your treatment will depend on what mental health issues you have and how it is affecting your daily life. It might include talking therapies such as counselling and cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), online self-management support and medicines if you want them.

    Find out more about treatment for mental health conditions

  4. Step 4 Moving on to adult mental health services

    If you turn 18 and still need mental health support, you will move on to adult mental health services. This is a time where you may need extra support and information.

    Find out more about moving on to adult mental health services

Getting support while you’re waiting for treatment

If you are referred for NHS help, there may be a waiting time before you are seen by, or hear from, the service.

This can be frustrating if you are struggling with how you are feeling, or if you are a parent or carer and want your child or young person to get help sooner.

But there are lots of things you can do while you’re waiting, including doing things to help yourself (self-help) and contacting charity services for support

Find out what to do to manage your mental health while you are waiting
