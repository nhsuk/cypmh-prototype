If you’ve been referred to your local children and adolescent mental health services (CAMHS), they will work with you to understand what support you need.

If you are a parent or carer, and your child under 16, you may be asked to be involved in these discussions, but in some cases CAMHS may suggest they speak to your child alone.

For some services you may also be able to refer yourself - or your parent or carer can refer you.

If you are a child or young person

A GP can talk to you, or your parent or carer, about what options are available to you in your area and refer you to your local children and adolescent mental health services (CAMHS).

Speaking to a GP about your mental health can seem scary, but they are there to listen to you and help you. It can help to think about what you want to say beforehand and write down some notes.

Make sure you tell the GP as much information as you can about:

  • how you’ve been feeling and what you’ve been experiencing
  • how long you been feeling like or experiencing this
  • how it’s affecting your day-to-day life

It’s really important to be as honest as you can. This will help the GP get a better idea of the support you need.

If you are under 16, you may need your parent or carer’s consent to be referred.

Young Minds has more information about how to speak to your GP about your mental health

If you are a parent or carer

You can speak to a GP with, or on behalf of, your child. The GP can give you more information about different types of support and services that are available locally. They may want to speak to your child before referring them to a mental health service, but this is not always essential.

Young Minds has more information about how to speak to a GP about your child's mental health

If you can’t, or don’t want to, talk to a GP, there are ways you can be referred to mental health services

You can refer yourself (self-referral)

You can often refer yourself, or your parent or carer can refer you.

To find out if you can refer yourself to a service, you’ll need to contact your local children and adolescent mental health services (CAMHS).

The contact details will be on your local CAMHS website, which you can find by searching online for ‘CAMHS’ and the county or town you live in, for example ‘CAMHS Kent’.

They will usually have a telephone number, email address or form to fill in.

They might ask for information such as:

  • what mental health difficulties you are having
  • how it is affecting your daily life
  • how long it has been going on and if it is getting worse?
  • what you have already tried and what helps

Try and give as much information at you can, even if it is difficult or feels very personal. The information you give will help make sure you get the support you need.

You can speak to other professionals

You can ask other people to refer you, such as:

  • a teacher
  • a school nurse
  • a youth worker
  • a social worker, if you already have one

Once you have a referral, or have referred yourself, you should be told how long it will take to hear from the service.

If you do not get this information, it can be helpful to ask so that you know what to expect.

There is likely to be a waiting list, and how long you need to wait will depend on the area that you live in. There is usually only a short wait to be contacted for a first appointment to understand your needs, but there may be a longer wait before you start receiving support.

This can be frustrating if you feel you need support sooner, but there are things you can do to help yourself while you wait.

If you haven’t heard anything after a few weeks, you can try contacting the service. Ask the person who referred you for the contact details, if you don’t have them.

If there is going to be a wait for support, it can be helpful to have people around you that you can talk to who are aware that you are experiencing difficulties.

There are also lots of charities that provide direct support such as counselling and helplines. Read more about charity and other mental health support.

If your situation changes or gets worse, then call or email the service you were referred to, to let them know what has changed. Or get urgent help if you need it.

Self-help techniques can also be a good way to manage your feelings or worries while you wait to be seen. Different things work for different people, but might include activities like listening to podcasts, using mental health apps, doing some exercise and being kind to yourself.

The charity the Anna Freud Centre and Every mind matters have lots of ideas.

If you can’t get a referral, you can ask a different GP or teacher or find out who else can refer you. You can also try referring yourself, if you haven’t already.

It’s important to give the person referring you as much information as you can about how you’ve been feeling or what you’ve been experiencing.

If they can’t refer you to your local children and young people’s mental health services, there may be other services that can help.

If you still cannot get a referral or the support you need, then there are people who may be able to speak up for you or give you advice. This is called advocacy or being an advocate.

You can find out more about advocacy from:

  • Mind – a mental health charity providing information and support.
  • Patients advice and liaison services (PALS) - an NHS service providing confidential advice and support which you can contact through your local hospital.

You can also do your own research into other services.

  • There is a youth wellbeing directory on the Anna Freud charity website.
  • You can search for local services on the Youth Access website
